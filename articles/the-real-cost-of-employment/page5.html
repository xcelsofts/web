The Bad Hire

Many businesses are pressured to fill vacancies in their workforce quickly as these vacancies can mean significant productivity and profit losses. This pressure can often result in a hurried hire that may not be the right fit for the job and an inevitable dismissal and restart of the whole process. The situation is worse for small businesses and startups who do not possess the resources to sustain a thorough hiring process.

Jorgen Sundberg, a well-known recruiter and founder of LinkHumans, has estimated the real cost of onboarding to be $240,000 per employee. He also suggests that recruiting the wrong employing, considering return on investment, hiring, total compensation, severance pay, lega fees and other costs totals more than $840,000 (based on hiring a mid-level manager who works 2.5 years then is terminated and replaced). While we tend to be much more conservative in our estimations, paying careful considerations to the cost of onboarding and including them in the real cost of employment is important.

We estimate the cost of onboarding to be 10% which we will add to the “loaded and compensated” rate bringing us to 49%. Of course, this may vary depending on your organization and the roles being fulfilled.

The Obvious Conclusion

There is nothing more to our conclusion other than it is much more expensive to hire employees than you might otherwise have thought and it might be a factor in helping you decide whether or not it is worth it to create new roles in your organization. We have taken a wide set of the most common expenses and eventualities to calculate and estimate “the real cost” or the “loaded and compensated” rate which is a factor that you can apply to a base wage to estimate the real cost of hiring any given worker.

Our resulting “loaded and compensated” rate came in at 49% which is in line with most other estimations you’ll find elsewhere. So, our fictitious junior web developer that we hired at $50,000 per year will actually cost us approximately $79,500. Imagine how quickly this number bloats for more experienced/educated workers and management with much higher salaries.

Remember, you should take care to calculate your own estimations based on your specific circumstances - don’t take our estimation for granted because your situation may be very different from the imagined scenario we have illustrated for example purposes.

Taking the time to estimate your real costs of hiring will, at least, give you an opportunity to make a better decision about hiring and perhaps looking at other alternatives to in-house employment.
